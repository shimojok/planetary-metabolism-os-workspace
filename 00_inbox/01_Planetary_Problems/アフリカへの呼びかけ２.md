### **Africa Vision: Addressing Climate Change and Food Security through AGRIX Project**

The greatest challenge we face today is climate change and its profound impact on food security. Frequent typhoons, heavy rainfall, heatwaves, and cold waves are causing extreme weather events such as floods, droughts, frost damage, and wildfires. These disasters severely affect food production, water resources, energy supply, infrastructure, public health, and economic activities. As a result, food and material prices are skyrocketing, medical costs are increasing, and enormous disaster recovery expenses are straining national budgets, exacerbating economic, insurance, and financial risks.

The root cause of climate change is the increase in greenhouse gases, driven by human economic activities such as food production and fossil fuel consumption. These activities have excessively depleted soil organic carbon, raising atmospheric CO₂ levels. Estimates suggest that the amount of carbon released due to soil organic matter loss from prehistoric times to the present is more than twice the carbon released from fossil fuel consumption. Through agriculture, deforestation, and urban development, we have extracted vast amounts of soil carbon, disrupting the carbon cycle, degrading soil, and reducing biomass. However, this also means that by restoring degraded soil, we can sequester a significant amount of carbon in the soil.

Globally, soil up to a depth of 2 meters stores between **1,500 and 2,400 gigatons (Gt) of carbon**, most of which originates from organic compounds synthesized by plants and microorganisms. The amount of carbon stored in soil organic matter (SOM) is approximately **three times the carbon present in the atmosphere as CO₂ and about four times the carbon stored in plant biomass**. Additionally, over 80% of the nitrogen, which is essential for plant growth, is stored in SOM. This makes SOM the largest reservoir of carbon and nitrogen in terrestrial ecosystems.

The incredible biodiversity of Earth exists because microorganisms, fungi, plants, and animals drive energy metabolism and material cycles. Among them, microorganisms—due to their vast diversity, population size, metabolic functions, and adaptability—are the key players in maintaining the Earth’s system.
