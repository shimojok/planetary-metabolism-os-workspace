Nigeria and Ethiopia, both possessing forest resources in the African continent, are grappling with the reduction and degradation of their forests. The primary causes include conversion of land to agriculture and pasture due to population growth, excessive logging and grazing, as well as factors like fires and pests. These issues adversely affect soil quality and productivity.

Soil degradation impacts forest growth and afforestation, manifesting as reduced organic matter, nutrient imbalance, erosion, salinity, chemical changes like acidification or alkalinization, and physical alterations in density and aeration. These changes diminish plant root development and water absorption, hindering access to essential water and nutrients for plant growth and survival. Soil degradation also reduces biodiversity and compromises ecosystem services.

Climate change also influences forest growth and afforestation. Variations in climate elements such as temperature, precipitation, drought, wind speed, and sunlight affect physiological processes like photosynthesis, respiration, transpiration, flowering, and fruiting in plants. Climate change induces shifts in climate and vegetation zones, impacting plant distribution and species composition. Moreover, it intensifies biological stressors like pests and invasive species, and increases the frequency and intensity of natural disasters like fires and floods, leading to forest damage and destruction.

In Japan, instances of forest collapse due to acid rain and heat stress have been addressed effectively through the application of liquid microbial materials on tree bark simultaneously with soil improvement. This approach has contributed to the recovery of pine and cedar trees.
