Modern agriculture, which aims to increase yield and production efficiency, imposes a huge cost on the global environment. One of them is global soil degradation. Soil degradation is caused by intensive agriculture and crop rotation disorders due to salt accumulation. The use of large amounts of fertilizer causes pests and crop diseases, reduces productivity significantly, reduces farmers’ income, and may lose productive farmland.

Today, more than 30% of the world’s soil is degraded by large-scale intensive agriculture. Many research institutions report that if this continues, the soil degradation rate will reach 90% by 2050. If this continues, more farmland will be abandoned and there will be a shortage of farmland. Many places are showing a decline in environmental resilience.

Degraded soil has little organic matter, little microbial activity, and low carbon fixation rates, making it the biggest cause of greenhouse gas problems, which I have warned.

Even in modern times, where technological innovation is advancing in many fields, why couldn’t agriculture solve such important problems? The reason is that they did not understand the “basic ecological processes” including the ecological hypercycle. This is because many farmers do not understand and practice all the plant and animal ecosystems based on the energy metabolism of microorganisms.

We can dramatically improve degraded soils in various environments. As a result of examining many agricultural technologies for soil improvement, we found a difference in approach and an essential common point. Whether it meets the “seven requirements” to realize the “basic ecological process”.

About 10 years ago, when I was researching the ecological process of soil, I found a research paper written by Dr. David M Wilkinson (University of Lincoln, UK.) “Fundamental Processes in Ecology: An earth systems approach”. [https://academic.oup.com/book/4978](https://academic.oup.com/book/4978)

"The book suggests that seven ecological processes are fundamental (not including natural selection and competition, which characterize all of life rather than only ecology): 

1. Energy flow (energy consumption and waste product excretion), 
2. Multiple guilds (autotrophs, decomposers, and parasites), 
3. Trade offs (specialization versus generalization, leading to biodiversity within guilds), 
4. Ecological hypercycles (cycles within cycles), 
5. Merging of organismal and ecological physiology (as life spreads over the planet, biotic and abiotic processes interact so strongly as to be inseparable), 
6. Photosynthesis (which it suggests is likely in most biospheres but not inevitable), 
7. Carbon sequestration. 
    
These fundamental processes lead to the emergence of nutrient cycling. The integration of Earth System Science with ecology is vitally important if ecological science is to successfully contribute to the massive problems and future challenges associated with global change. The book is heavily influenced by Lovelock's Gaia hypothesis.
