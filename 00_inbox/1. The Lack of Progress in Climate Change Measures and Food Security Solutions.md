## 1. The Lack of Progress in Climate Change Measures and Food Security Solutions

#### Lack of Understanding of Carbon Cycling and Technological Superiority

One of the greatest challenges we face today is **climate change**. The increasing frequency of extreme weather events—typhoons, torrential rains, heatwaves, and cold spells—has triggered **floods, droughts, crop failures, and wildfires**. These disasters severely impact **food production, infrastructure, human health, and economic activities**, leading to **rising food and material costs, increased medical expenses, and mounting disaster recovery costs**, which further strain national finances.

Despite a clear understanding of these challenges, **why has progress in climate change mitigation remained slow?** The primary reason is a **lack of understanding of the carbon cycle mechanisms and the absence of adequate technological solutions**.

The **root cause** of climate change is the **rise in greenhouse gas emissions**, driven by human economic activities such as **agriculture and fossil fuel production**, which have **excessively depleted soil carbon reserves** and increased **atmospheric CO₂ concentrations**. Studies estimate that the **carbon released due to soil degradation since prehistoric times is more than twice the amount emitted by fossil fuel consumption**. Over centuries, **agriculture and deforestation have extracted vast amounts of soil organic matter, leading to severe soil degradation**. However, this also presents **a major opportunity**—**restoring degraded soil can sequester a significant amount of carbon back into the land**.

Globally, the **top two meters of soil store 1,500–2,400 gigatons (Gt) of carbon**, primarily in the form of **soil organic matter (SOM)**, derived from the biosynthesis of plants and microorganisms. The **amount of carbon stored in soil** is approximately **three times that of atmospheric CO₂** and **four times that of plant biomass**. Additionally, over **80% of nitrogen**—the most essential nutrient for plant growth—exists within **SOM in terrestrial ecosystems**. In other words, **SOM serves as the largest carbon and nitrogen reservoir on land**.

Earth's remarkable **biological diversity** is driven by **microbial, fungal, and plant-animal metabolic cycles**, facilitating the circulation of essential elements. Among them, **microorganisms play the central role in Earth’s systems**, possessing **unparalleled diversity, population size, metabolic functions, and adaptive capabilities**.

A **systems approach** to addressing climate change must incorporate fundamental ecological processes, including the **microbial-driven "ecological hypercycle" and carbon cycling**, which align with **sustainable agricultural systems**. By leveraging **soil microbial diversity**, we have the potential to **combat global warming**. The **restoration of soil health**—through **replenishment of soil organic matter and microbial communities**—is not only a crucial **climate change countermeasure** but also the foundation of **sustainable agriculture and material cycling**.

#### The Root Causes: Rising Material Costs and Low Agricultural Productivity

While the **global population continues to grow**, **food security has become more fragile than ever**. Climate change negatively affects **crop growth conditions**, resulting in **lower yields and higher production costs**. The **decline in global food supply capacity** carries enormous **social and economic consequences**. **Food shortages and price spikes** disproportionately impact **developing countries**, exacerbating **hunger, poverty, and political instability**. Furthermore, **food waste and inefficient distribution systems** further weaken global food security.

Despite substantial financial investments from **international organizations** to support food aid and agricultural development, **why have these efforts failed to produce significant results?** The primary reason is **the lack of productive agricultural inputs and suitable farmland**.

The **core issue of the food crisis** lies in:

1. **Declining soil functionality**
2. **High costs of agricultural inputs**
3. **Fragmentation of agricultural production and distribution networks**
4. **Lack of a global strategy for food security**

Food production is both a **cost of maintaining a stable society** and a **catalyst for economic growth**. We must focus on **enhancing agricultural productivity, fostering technological innovation, and creating interconnected agricultural information networks**. Given the **challenges posed by climate change**, we need to establish a **global food system** that promotes **multilateral food security cooperation**.

Instead of a **competitive scramble for food procurement** led by **national governments and major food corporations**, the global community must develop a **coordinated framework** for **local food production, food reserves, and food distribution** to ensure **fair and sustainable access to resources**.

#### MBT55 & MBT Sustainable Cycle: Solutions for Soil Restoration and Carbon Sequestration

Our research has demonstrated that **MBT55 (Multi-Biotechnology Microbial Consortium) and the MBT Sustainable Cycle (Organic Decomposition System)** can **restore degraded soils, enhance carbon and nutrient cycling, and improve agricultural productivity**.

The **integration of Earth System Science and Ecology** is essential for addressing **climate change and sustainable food production**. By focusing on **carbon cycling and sequestration**, we can develop an effective **framework for climate change mitigation** while ensuring **long-term food security**.
