### Opinions of experts on aid to Africa

I strongly feel your passion and concern regarding the various issues in Africa, including the food problem, as well as your expectations for the agricultural project you are proposing. Your frank observations about the lack of progress in discussions and the noticeable victim mentality are acknowledged as your direct experiences from working on the ground.
Regarding the points you raised, I would like to consider them from several perspectives.
#### 1. Regarding the Awareness of Problem-Solving

While Africa is often spoken of as a single entity, the reality is that social structures, cultures, and historical backgrounds differ significantly between countries and regions. Therefore, the awareness of problem-solving is also likely diverse.
 * Background of Victim Mentality: There may be a perception in some quarters that difficult situations are caused by external factors, such as the long history of colonial rule and the recent impact of climate change. This could stem from a feeling that it is difficult to solve problems through their own efforts alone.
 * Aspects Appearing as a Lack of Ownership: There might be a tendency to expect external support due to a history of aid and the experience that past assistance has not always led to sustainable development. Additionally, difficulties in accessing information and educational levels may make it challenging to identify concrete solutions.
 * Potential Motivation: On the other hand, it is also true that many people aspire to a better life and are taking action to achieve it. Entrepreneurs exist, and there are movements in various places actively working to solve local issues. However, these activities may not be easily communicated externally, or they may require financial and technical support.
#### 2. Regarding the Thoughts of Government Agencies

Government agencies in Africa are also not monolithic. Agricultural policies and development strategies vary greatly depending on the country and administration.
 * National Development Plans: In many countries, food security and the modernization of agriculture are positioned as important national development goals. However, there are differences in the specific strategies and implementation capabilities for achieving these goals.
 * Resource Allocation: Within limited budgets, policies must be advanced while considering the balance with other sectors (infrastructure development, education, healthcare, etc.) besides agriculture. Therefore, investment in the agricultural sector may sometimes feel insufficient.
 * Attitude towards International Cooperation: Support from Japan and other countries is a significant source of development funds for governments. However, the methods of utilization and priorities are likely subject to various discussions and adjustments within the government. The needs on the ground may not always be directly reflected.
 * Expectations for Technology Transfer: At the government level, there is likely an expectation for the introduction of overseas technology and human resource development. However, when it comes to specific project proposals and negotiations, the capabilities of the responsible departments, information gathering abilities, and political decisions can have an impact, and it may take time.
#### 3. Regarding the Comparison with China

China's support for Africa is primarily focused on infrastructure investment and resource development, driven by economic interests and implemented on a large scale under government leadership. On the other hand, Japan's support, centered on ODA (Official Development Assistance), involves cooperation in a wider range of fields.
 * Differences in Approach: China tends to have a top-down approach with rapid decision-making and execution, while Japan tends to respect the needs of the partner country and build relationships over time.
 * Requests for Technical Cooperation: In the case of China, they often inject their own technology and labor into large-scale projects, and it may be less common for African countries to request individual technical cooperation. On the other hand, Japan also implements technical cooperation projects, but there may be insufficient information dissemination or a mismatch with the needs of the African side.
#### 4. Regarding Collaboration with Agricultural Organizations
Agricultural organizations play an important role in conveying the opinions of farmers to the government and disseminating new technologies.
 * Organizational Strength and Information Dissemination: The organizational strength and information dissemination capabilities of agricultural organizations in Africa vary by country and region. If they are not functioning adequately, information about new technologies may not reach farmers at the grassroots level.
 * Leadership: The leadership of the organizations and their understanding of new technologies are also important factors in promoting technology transfer.

**To Advance Your Project**
To break through the current situation and advance your project, you might consider the following approaches:
 * Reconfirming Local Needs and Conducting Thorough Interviews: To gain a deeper understanding of the true challenges behind the victim mentality and the potential needs, it is important to engage in dialogue with various stakeholders (government officials, agricultural organizations, farmers, local residents, etc.).
 * Introducing Success Stories and Presenting Concrete Benefits: It is important to clearly communicate past success stories and the specific results that can be expected from your technology. Presenting numerical targets may also be effective.
 * Implementing Pilot Projects: Implementing a pilot project on a small scale first and demonstrating its results can make it easier to gain the understanding and cooperation of stakeholders.
 * Building Local Partnerships: Collaborating with reliable local partners (research institutions, NGOs, companies, etc.) can not only facilitate the smooth implementation of the project but also enhance its sustainability.
 * Strengthening Information Dissemination: Actively disseminating information about the purpose and content of the project, as well as the expected effects, through local languages and media can raise interest and lead to inquiries.
 * Collaborating with the Japanese Government and International Organizations: Referring to examples of support from the Japanese government and exploring potential collaborations could be effective. It is also worth investigating agricultural support programs implemented by international organizations.

Solving the challenges in Africa requires not only external support but also proactive efforts from the local people. Persistently continuing dialogue, building trust, and demonstrating an attitude of working together to solve problems may be key to breaking through the current situation.

### "Fundamental Processes in Ecology"

Over the past five years, I have collected and reviewed many reports and technical explanations on African agricultural development conducted by the World Bank and FAO in the past. These agricultural development projects are based on extensive research and are impressive in their content. However, due to the recent impacts of climate change, the results towards achieving net zero have been limited, and problems such as farmland degradation due to flooding, nutrition, and food security continue to worsen.

In the 1950s, Japan was called a “pollution paradise” due to widespread air, water, marine, and soil pollution. However, thanks to the Japanese people's technological innovation and perseverance, the situation was successfully recovered. Furthermore, in the aftermath of the Great East Japan Earthquake on March 11, 2011, the massive tsunami brought large amounts of seabed sludge onto vast agricultural land. Many farmers lamented the salt damage and soil degradation, abandoned their farmland, and gave up farming. However, we entered the field immediately after the disaster, using the MBT55 microbial consortium to compost the sludge and achieved high yields.

What was the difference between the farmers and municipalities that gave up and those that restored their soil?

Most farmers and municipal agricultural departments did not understand the mechanisms of ecology, carbon cycling, global organic matter cycles, microbes, and the hypercycle. It was as if they only knew how to use the three major nutrients in human nutrition and couldn’t comprehend the usefulness of gut bacteria, metabolic mechanisms, and metabolites. It was a very poor situation. Even now, they scatter sodium hypochlorite over contaminated soil, turning it into a haven for flies.

Perhaps the World Bank and FAO are in a similar situation. At this rate, Climate Finance will lose its sustainability, and all the substantial funds will be wasted. African agricultural development must not only solve nutrition and food issues and improve resilience, but also function as a food system that contributes to both the domestic and international markets.

I am planning the AGRIX Project to solve Africa's issues within three years, develop the agricultural economies of African nations, and build a multilateral food security system that no country has ever considered. The AGRIX Project will realize the composting of food waste and livestock excrement, the domestic production of fertilizers, an increase in yields (50% to 3 times), a reduction in production costs (40%), a reduction in food loss through crops with better freshness retention, and a reduction in medical expenses.

The carbon dioxide emissions from microbial metabolism are part of the carbon cycle and do not contribute to increased CO2 emissions. MBT55 not only promotes carbon sequestration, but also long-term carbon sequestration through the creation of humus, increases plant root mass, and increases the amount of suberin and biomass, which store carbon. And the increase in carbon leads to higher crop yields.

In the past, economists emphasized the trade-off between environmental conservation and economic growth, which accelerated environmental degradation. However, we can use the hypercycle to transform cost structures and achieve both improved environmental conservation and agricultural economic development.

Over the past few years, I have sent the AGRIX White Paper to the World Bank, FAO, IFAD, and other experts, but many people do not read it because it is long. The reason is that it is outside their area of expertise. However, unless we implement these ecological mechanisms, we cannot move forward with global warming countermeasures.

In addition, I, coming from the insurance and IT industries, have devised the BioValley Plan to connect Climate Finance to sustainable agricultural development. This will attract the attention of insurance companies, investment firms, food companies, and institutional investors worldwide.

### African agricultural development and food security measures by the World Bank, FAO and African agricultural organizations

Efforts by the World Bank, FAO, and African Agricultural Organizations for African Agricultural Development and Food Security Measures
The World Bank, the FAO (Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations), and African agricultural organizations each play different roles and have distinct strengths as they work to address agricultural development and food security issues in Africa. Here is a summary of their specific efforts:

#### 1. The World Bank

The World Bank supports the transformation of Africa's agricultural sector through loans, grants, technical assistance, and knowledge sharing. Key initiatives include:
 * Strategic Investment: Under the "Feed Africa" strategy, the World Bank aims to invest $24 billion in African agriculture over ten years, starting in 2015. This is intended to improve productivity, strengthen supply chains, and enhance market access.
 * Climate Change Measures: Promoting Climate-Smart Agriculture to enhance the resilience of farmers against extreme weather events such as droughts and floods. This includes sustainable land management, efficient water management, and the introduction of climate-adapted crops.
 * Promotion of Digital Agriculture: Supporting the adoption of data-driven digital agriculture to improve farmers' decision-making abilities and efficiency. This includes soil mapping, precision agriculture technologies, and the provision of market information.
 * Food Security and Nutrition: Strengthening food and nutrition security by supporting information sharing, rapid resource provision, and the design of long-term reforms needed for resilient food and nutrition systems.
 * Mobilizing the Private Sector: Encouraging private investment in the agriculture and food sectors by supporting the design, piloting, and de-risking of innovative public-private partnerships (PPPs).
 * Soil Health and Access to Fertilizers: Partnering with the OCP Group to support five million farmers in West Africa and the Sahel by promoting the use of customized fertilizers and sustainable farming practices to improve soil health and agricultural productivity.

**Specific Project Examples:**
 * Regional Agriculture Flagship Programs: Supporting programs where multiple countries collaborate to address key agricultural challenges in specific regions.
 * Smallholder Farmer Support Projects: Providing smallholder farmers with access to finance, technical guidance, and market linkages to improve their income and food security.

#### 2. FAO (Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations)

As a specialized international agency for food and agriculture, FAO collaborates with African governments, regional bodies, and other partners to improve food security and achieve sustainable agriculture. Key initiatives include:
 * Policy and Technical Assistance: Supporting the formulation and implementation of national agricultural policies and promoting the adoption of sustainable agricultural technologies and practices. This includes soil management, water resource management, pest and disease management, and reduction of post-harvest losses.
 * Provision of Food Security Information: Supporting the monitoring of food supply and demand and the development of early warning systems to enhance the capacity to respond to food crises.
 * Emergency Assistance: Providing emergency food assistance and agricultural rehabilitation support in the event of food crises caused by natural disasters or conflicts.
 * "One Country One Priority Product (OCOP)" Initiative: Supporting countries to formalize and boost the production, processing, and marketing of their chosen priority agricultural products through more sustainable 'green' value chains to contribute to economic growth and food security.
 * Nutrition Improvement: Supporting the improvement of nutrition in Africa through the strengthening of nutrition policies, the promotion of healthy diets, and the improvement of access to food.
 * Adaptation to and Mitigation of Climate Change: Supporting efforts to reduce the impact of climate change on agriculture by introducing adaptation measures and reducing greenhouse gas emissions.

**Specific Examples of Initiatives:**
 * Addressing climate-related livestock losses and livelihood strains in the Sahel region: Working with the Intergovernmental Authority on Development (IGAD) to implement emergency support projects for pastoralists and agro-pastoralists affected by climate change in East Africa.
 * Supporting the strengthening of nutrition policies: Assisting countries in enhancing policy and planning instruments for nutrition, healthy diets, and the right to adequate food.

#### 3. African Agricultural Organizations

Various agricultural organizations exist in Africa at regional, national, and farmer levels, each playing its respective role.
 * Regional Agricultural Organizations:
   * Eastern Africa Farmers Federation (EAFF): Represents the voices of farmers in the East Africa region, advocating for policies, sharing information, and providing capacity development.
   * West African Farmers' and Producers' Organizations (ROPPA): Networks farmers' organizations in the West Africa region, promoting policy dialogue and improved market access.
 * National-Level Agricultural Organizations: National farmer unions and agricultural cooperatives implement activities such as advocating for farmers' rights, providing technical guidance, supporting market access, and facilitating the joint purchase of inputs.
 * Thematic Agricultural Organizations: Organizations exist to support specific crops, livestock, organic farming, and women farmers, providing specialized knowledge and technology.

**Specific Examples of Initiatives:**
 * Sasakawa Africa Association (SAA): Supported by the Nippon Foundation, SAA has been providing agricultural assistance in 17 African countries for over 35 years. Currently focusing on Ethiopia, Mali, Nigeria, and Uganda, it supports smallholder farmers' food, nutrition, and income security through agricultural extension services, reduction of post-harvest losses, promotion of conservation agriculture, dissemination of high-nutrient crops, and agribusiness development.
 * One Acre Fund: An NGO operating in Kenya, Rwanda, and Burundi, providing smallholder farmers with access to financing, training, and high-quality seeds to increase yields and improve incomes.
 * African Development Foundation (ADF): Implemented a project in Ghana focused on improving cassava production, contributing to increased farmer income and local economic development.

**Collaboration and Coordination**

These institutions and organizations not only work independently but also collaborate and coordinate with each other to address agricultural development and food security issues more effectively. For example, the World Bank and FAO may jointly conduct research and analysis or implement joint projects. Furthermore, collaboration between international organizations and regional/national agricultural organizations is crucial for implementing activities at the grassroots level.
Agricultural development and food security measures in Africa are complex challenges that require the cooperation and continuous efforts of many stakeholders. By leveraging the strengths of each institution and organization and strengthening collaboration, greater results can be expected.

Agriculture is the backbone of many African economies, making significant contributions to GDP, being a major source of foreign exchange, and the primary supplier of industrial raw materials.

 * Promotion of Digital Agriculture: The World Bank supports the introduction of data-driven digital agriculture, with the initial adoption of soil mapping and precision agriculture technologies.

 * Improving Market Access for Smallholder Farmers: The FAO's "Hand-in-Hand Initiative" aims to connect small-scale farmers with markets and resources, helping farmers in Ethiopia and Bangladesh, among others, to sell their products at better prices.
 * SHEP (Smallholder Horticultural Empowerment and Promotion) Approach: The SAA supports the strengthening of business skills and the fostering of entrepreneurship among smallholder horticultural farmers.

#### 5. Technical Improvement of African Agricultural Workers:
 * Implementation of Training Programs: Many agricultural organizations and international bodies conduct training programs on agricultural techniques, nutrition, and gender issues to enhance the knowledge and skills of farmers.
 * Training of Extension Workers: The SAA emphasizes capacity building by educating and training extension workers at agricultural universities.

#### 6. Increase in Income:
 * Income Growth through Increased Yields: As demonstrated by the One Acre Fund, increased yields resulting from appropriate support directly contribute to higher farmer incomes.
 * Income Growth through Improved Market Access: The FAO's initiatives and support for market linkages by NGOs enable farmers to sell under more favorable conditions, leading to income increases.

#### 7. Changes in Awareness of African Producers and Food Companies:
 * Growing Interest in Sustainable Agriculture: With increasing awareness of environmental issues and climate change, more producers and food companies are becoming interested in sustainable agriculture.
 * Focus on Nutrient-Rich Crops: Initiatives like the collaboration between SAA and HarvestPlus to promote biofortified crops are raising awareness about the importance of nutrition improvement.
 * Recognition of the Need for Fair Trade Models: Discussions aimed at reshaping partnerships between African and European food systems are leading to a growing recognition of the need for fairer global trade models.

#### 8. Impact on African Society:
 * Improved Food Security: Increased productivity and better market access are strengthening regional and national food security.
 * Poverty Reduction: Increased agricultural income significantly contributes to poverty reduction in rural areas.
 * Job Creation: The development of agriculture-related businesses is creating new employment opportunities, especially for young people.
 * Improved Nutritional Status: The promotion of nutrient-rich crops and nutrition education is improving people's nutritional status.
 * Strengthening of Communities: Support for the establishment of agricultural cooperatives and other initiatives strengthens collaboration among farmers and enhances community resilience.

#### Important Considerations:
 * These outcomes vary by region and project, and progress is not uniform across all areas.
 * The availability of data and differences in measurement methods can make it challenging to provide specific numerical figures in all cases.
 * The policies of national governments and the efforts of farmers themselves, in addition to external support, significantly influence these outcomes.
