When I asked FAO about the causes and countermeasures, they responded that they are researching the matter and aim to develop solutions by 2030. However, if they do not yet understand the essential mechanisms of soil degradation and its improvement, I believe even development will be difficult.

Currently, the cause of the global food issue lies in the relative decline in productivity. Yields from degraded farmland have dropped significantly, and even on high-yield farmland, large amounts of agricultural inputs are being used. The production and use of chemical fertilizers contribute greatly to greenhouse gas emissions. This is especially because nitrogen, the main component of chemical fertilizers, remains in the soil and turns into nitrous oxide (N₂O), which has about 300 times the greenhouse effect of carbon dioxide (CO₂). Farmers around the world use large amounts of expensive chemical fertilizers, and those costs are reflected in food prices.

Excessive use of chemical fertilizers increases residual fertilizer in the soil, raising concerns about groundwater contamination and impacts on ecosystems. In addition, the nitrogen components that plants fail to absorb remain as nitrate nitrogen, and when combined with meat products, may generate carcinogenic substances, which is a matter of concern.

The world is still at such a level. Why is the understanding of agricultural technology not spreading? We solved these very issues more than 30 years ago.

More than 25 years ago, while conducting research on functional materials and functional herbal ingredients, I worked under Mr. Yutaka Hanamoto, who had developed outstanding agricultural technologies. My goal was to learn the biotechnology and mineral knowledge he possessed. Afterwards, I compared more than 20 types of agricultural technologies, studied their mechanisms, and among them, focused on two technologies. One of them is MBT55.

The Hanamoto Agricultual Method and NASARA Method have shown remarkable results, and MBT55 clarified the mechanism behind it.

When it comes to carbon sequestration and degraded soil, it is not simply a matter of applying compost. One must consider the quality of the compost and the cost of producing it.

Evaluations of carbon sequestration in the carbon credit market are often negative, and they do not take into account the importance of carbon flow (carbon cycling) or the ecological hypercycle. What is even more problematic is the high cost of compost production.

Our MBT55 turns food waste into humus in 24 hours. This difference is extremely significant.

In addition, organizations like the World Bank and the Rockefeller Foundation mainly support small-scale (family) farming in Africa. However, that is nothing more than food production for subsistence, and just like Japan’s agricultural policy that increased part-time farmers, it is bound to fail.
