In recent years, extreme weather events have become more frequent worldwide, leading to severe **flood damage** caused by torrential rains. At the same time, **water shortages due to drought** have become a critical issue, particularly in Africa, where **irrigation facilities** are being constructed in areas adjacent to flood-prone zones. This means that governments are **spending enormous amounts on flood recovery** while simultaneously **investing heavily in irrigation infrastructure**.

**The AquaGrid System system** not only diverts excessive rainwater through underground structures to **protect cities and farmland from frequent floods**, but also stores this water for **agriculture, industry, and drinking water use**. Additionally, advanced **fluid dynamics-based structures** enable **hydropower generation**.

This system distributes flood drainage inlets across a wide area, connects multiple regions via **a pipeline network**, and installs **hydropower generators** at **reservoirs and branching points**, helping to mitigate **urban heat islands**.

Another major objective of this project is to **redirect the enormous flood recovery costs into infrastructure investment**, turning it into a **large-scale public works initiative** that also drives **job creation**.

A **notable reference project** is **Japan’s the Metropolitan Outer Area Underground Discharge Channel**.

### 1. Economic Effect Calculation for the Metropolitan Outer Area Underground Discharge Channel
#### Assumptions and Background

- **Construction Cost:** Approximately 230 billion yen (this figure is specific to Japan)
- **Operation Record:** From 2006 to July 2021, there have been 131 flood control operations over roughly 15 years
- **Severe Flood Scenario in the Metropolitan Area:**
    - Fatalities: Several thousand
    - Evacuees: Over 5 million
    - Direct damage: Approximately 47 trillion yen
    - Economic losses: Approximately 731 trillion yen  
        → In an extreme scenario, total damages could reach around 778 trillion yen

#### Example Model Calculation

In reality, the damage per flood event can vary greatly. Here we assume a “moderate flood event” (not as catastrophic as a worst-case scenario, yet severe enough to cause significant damage):

1. **Assumptions for a Moderate Flood Event:**
    
    - If the flood were not controlled, the estimated damage per event might be:
        - Direct damage (to homes, vehicles, infrastructure, etc.): about 0.5 trillion yen
        - Indirect losses (economic shutdown, supply chain disruptions, etc.): about 3.0 trillion yen
        - **Total per event:** Approximately 3.5 trillion yen (this is a conservative estimate for moderate damage)
2. **For All 131 Events (No Countermeasure Scenario):**
    
    - Total estimated damages: 131 × 3.5 trillion yen = approximately 458.5 trillion yen
3. **Actual Effect with Flood Control (Damage Mitigation):**
    
    - Assume that the underground reservoir system reduces the damage by about 90% in each event. Then the actual damage incurred would be:  
        458.5 trillion yen × 10% = approximately 45.85 trillion yen over the period
4. **Damage Avoidance Effect:**
    
    - Avoided damage = 458.5 trillion yen – 45.85 trillion yen ≈ 412.65 trillion yen
    - (This is a conservative example assuming a series of moderate flood events.)

Furthermore, in the event of an actual “catastrophic disaster” (with potential damage of around 778 trillion yen per event), the avoidance effect would be several orders of magnitude higher, showing that the existence of such a system directly translates to a national-scale risk reduction.

**Summary:**

- The underground reservoir system, with a construction investment of 230 billion yen, may have avoided cumulative damages exceeding 400 trillion yen over 15 years.
- When averaged annually, this corresponds to roughly 27–28 trillion yen per year in avoided damages, indicating an extremely high return on investment.

### 2. Quantitative Risk-Hedging Impact for Insurance and Reinsurance Companies

From an insurance perspective, a single flood event can force enormous payouts that severely strain a company’s finances. Below is a model example based on the above calculations:

#### Assumptions

- Based on the previous model, assume that in the absence of countermeasures, the total expected damage over 15 years is about 458.5 trillion yen (as a conservative average for moderate flood events).
- With the underground reservoir system in place, damage is reduced by about 90%, resulting in actual damage of about 45.85 trillion yen over 15 years, or roughly 3.06 trillion yen per year.

#### Risk Impact Analysis

- **Expected Annual Loss Without Countermeasures:**  
    458.5 trillion yen ÷ 15 years ≈ 30.57 trillion yen per year
- **Actual Annual Loss With Countermeasures:**  
    Approximately 3.06 trillion yen per year
- **Damage Reduction:**  
    30.57 trillion yen – 3.06 trillion yen ≈ 27.51 trillion yen per year

#### Evaluation of Risk Hedging

- If an insurance company were responsible for covering such flood risks, the underground reservoir system would effectively avoid approximately 27–28 trillion yen in annual payouts.
- This represents roughly a 90% reduction in risk exposure, dramatically lowering the potential claim burden on both insurers and reinsurers.
- In financial markets, such risk reduction can be used to structure catastrophe bonds (cat bonds) or other insurance-linked securities, thereby enabling more stable asset management.

- **Revenue Distribution:**
    - The SPV uses revenue from hydropower generation, water usage fees, and avoided reconstruction costs to pay investors a fixed rate (e.g., as guaranteed by the Rothschild group) with any surplus reinvested to expand the project.
