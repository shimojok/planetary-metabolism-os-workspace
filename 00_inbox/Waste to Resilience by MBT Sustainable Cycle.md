## "The Earth's ecosystem is a self-organized circular biogeochemical system."

This system is constituted by a continuous network of **biogeochemical cycles** and **energy flux**, upon which all life activities are fundamentally based.
#### 1. Biogeochemical Cycles

The major cycles include:

- **Carbon Cycle**: Atmospheric CO₂ is fixed into organic matter through **photosynthesis** by plants, and is returned to the atmosphere via respiration, decomposition, and combustion by organisms.
- **Nitrogen Cycle**: Nitrogen circulates between inorganic and organic forms through **nitrogen fixation** by nitrogen-fixing bacteria, **nitrification** by nitrifying bacteria, and **denitrification** by denitrifying bacteria.
- **Phosphorus Cycle**: Inorganic phosphorus released from the weathering of rocks is assimilated by organisms and circulates as organic phosphorus compounds.
#### 2. Energy Flow

Solar energy is fixed by **primary producers** such as plants and algae (through photosynthesis), and flows through the ecosystem across **trophic levels**. Unlike matter, energy does not cycle but is eventually dissipated to the external environment as heat loss (entropy).
#### 3. Detrital System & Microbial Loop

**Organic detritus** such as dead organisms and excreta is **mineralized** by **decomposers** like bacteria and fungi and reused as inorganic nutrients. This process includes **humification**, which generates **humic substances** in the soil.
#### 4. Ecological Hypercycle

Natural ecosystems possess **multi-layered and diverse metabolic pathways**, forming **syntrophic networks** where specific microbial groups and metabolic pathways complement and enhance each other through cooperative metabolism. This enables ecosystems to maintain **resilience** and **self-regulation** in response to external disturbances.


To mimic and accelerate such a hypersystemic circulatory structure, it is not sufficient to merely apply superficial techniques like "no pesticide use" or "no-till farming." It is essential to develop technologies that **understand and promote the metabolic networks of ecosystems themselves (e.g., MBT55)**.

## Restoring Tsunami-Devastated Farmland through MBT55: Transforming Seabed Sludge into Fertile Soil

Following the Great East Japan Earthquake in 2011, vast areas of farmland were submerged by tsunami waves and buried under thick layers of seabed sludge. These layers—often 4 to 10 cm deep—carried high levels of salinity and toxic substances such as heavy metals and petroleum-derived compounds. As a result, many agricultural fields suffered from severe soil degradation, rendering crop production nearly impossible. This catastrophe not only damaged infrastructure, but also devastated the livelihoods of farming communities.

In this harsh environment, **MBT55**, a microbial humic compost, was applied as a rapid-response soil restoration solution. Within days of being lightly broadcast across the contaminated surface and activated by natural rainfall, the MBT55 microbial complex began decomposing organic matter in the sludge, **converting it into bioavailable nutrient salts** while simultaneously **detoxifying heavy metals and chemical pollutants**. By May 20, just three weeks after application, rice seedlings could be successfully transplanted in fields once deemed unusable. By June 18, visual comparison between treated and untreated plots showed stark contrasts: MBT55-treated fields exhibited vigorous rice growth, while untreated fields remained barren with sludge visibly intact.

**MBT55’s technical superiority** lies in its ability to initiate what we call an **Ecological Hypercycle**—a high-efficiency, multi-phase biochemical process powered by proprietary microbial consortia. This mechanism accelerates carbon and nitrogen cycling, enhances microbial biomass, stabilizes organic matter, and rebuilds soil structure with remarkable speed. It also breaks down harmful compounds through natural bioremediation pathways such as chelation, enzymatic reduction, and humification.

The **MBT Sustainable Cycle** extends this concept beyond agriculture. It forms an organic resource network connecting agriculture, livestock, food processing, food distribution, restaurants, forestry, schools, and government facilities. By transforming organic waste from each sector into high-value MBT55 compost, it drastically reduces disposal costs, promotes circular bioeconomy, and regenerates soil ecosystems—all while requiring minimal inputs and energy.

This clear and science-based mechanism is what makes the MBT Sustainable Cycle a powerful evolution of regenerative agriculture. It enhances regenerative practices with **speed, scalability, and ecological integrity**, supporting food security and climate resilience across regions and sectors.
