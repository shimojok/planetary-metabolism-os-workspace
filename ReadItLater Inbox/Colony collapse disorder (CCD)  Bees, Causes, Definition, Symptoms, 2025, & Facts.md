**colony collapse disorder (CCD)**, disorder affecting [honeybee](https://www.britannica.com/animal/honeybee) colonies that is [characterized](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/characterized) by sudden colony death, with a lack of healthy adult bees inside the hive. Although the cause is not known, researchers suspect that multiple factors may be involved. The disorder appears to affect the adult bees’ ability to navigate. They leave the hive to find [pollen](https://www.britannica.com/science/pollen) and never return. [Honey](https://www.britannica.com/topic/honey) and pollen are usually present in the hive, and there is often evidence of recent brood rearing. In some cases the queen and a small number of survivor bees may remain in the brood nest. CCD is also characterized by delayed robbing of the honey in the dead colonies by other, healthy [bee](https://www.britannica.com/animal/bee) colonies in the immediate area, as well as slower than normal invasion by common pests, such as [wax](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/wax) moths and small hive beetles. The disorder appears to affect only the [western honeybee](https://www.britannica.com/animal/western-honeybee) (*[Apis mellifera](https://www.britannica.com/animal/western-honeybee)*), and the phenomenon appears most severe in the United States.

## History of CCD and its economic impact

The unexplained loss of honeybee colonies that came to be known as CCD was first reported in the fall of 2006 by a commercial beekeeper from [Pennsylvania](https://www.britannica.com/place/Pennsylvania-state), U.S., who was overwintering his colonies in [Florida](https://www.britannica.com/place/Florida). (Subsequent investigations suggested that beekeepers had experienced unexplained colony losses for at least the previous three years; similar losses had been reported previously as well, including in the late 19th century and in various decades of the 20th century.) By February 2007 several large commercial migratory beekeeping operations in the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States) had reported cases of CCD, with some operators suffering the loss of 50–90 percent of their colonies. Many of these larger operations were overwintering their colonies in [California](https://www.britannica.com/place/California-state), Florida, [Oklahoma](https://www.britannica.com/place/Oklahoma-state), and [Texas](https://www.britannica.com/place/Texas-state). By late February 2007 some nonmigratory operations located in the mid-Atlantic region and the [Pacific Northwest](https://www.britannica.com/place/Northwest-region) of the United States also had reported the loss of more than 50 percent of their colonies. The absence of dead bees in the affected hives made initial investigations difficult and inconclusive. That same year, other countries, including [Canada](https://www.britannica.com/place/Canada), [Portugal](https://www.britannica.com/place/Portugal), [Italy](https://www.britannica.com/place/Italy), [Spain](https://www.britannica.com/place/Spain), [Greece](https://www.britannica.com/place/Greece), [Germany](https://www.britannica.com/place/Germany), [Poland](https://www.britannica.com/place/Poland), [France](https://www.britannica.com/place/France), and [Switzerland](https://www.britannica.com/place/Switzerland), also reported substantial losses of honeybees. From 2006 to 2011 total annual colony losses in the United States averaged around 33 percent; beekeepers [cited](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/cited) CCD as the cause of roughly one-third of those losses.

In 2025 entomologists from [Washington State University](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Washington-State-University), using annual survey data from beekeepers across the country, announced that commercial honey bee colony losses in the United States were projected to reach between 60 percent and 70 percent that year. Given that losses from the previous decade typically ranged between 40–50 percent, this significant uptick may signal the biggest loss of western honeybee colonies in U.S. history.

[Beekeeper](https://cdn.britannica.com/84/115384-050-DA033C29/Beekeepers-pollination-service-crops.jpg)Beekeepers provide pollination service for more than 100 crops grown commercially in the United States.

[Beekeeping](https://www.britannica.com/topic/beekeeping) is a critical component of modern agriculture. CCD not only threatens the beekeeping operations that provide [pollination](https://www.britannica.com/science/pollination) service and honey production but also has the potential for crippling the production of the many [crops](https://www.britannica.com/topic/crop-agriculture) that are dependent on honeybees for pollination. In the United States, beekeepers provide pollination service for more than 100 commercially grown crops, including many [fruits](https://www.britannica.com/science/fruit-plant-reproductive-body) and [vegetables](https://www.britannica.com/topic/vegetable). The economic [value](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/value) of U.S. crops that benefit from bee pollination has been estimated at $18 billion annually. In 2022 the California [almond](https://www.britannica.com/plant/almond) export crop alone was valued at $4.7 billion and required more than two million bee colonies for pollination (out of a total of about 2.7 million colonies in the United States)—the largest coordinated pollination event in the world. With the number of available colonies for crop pollination in the country in decline, the beekeeping industry faced a tremendous challenge in meeting the demand for pollination services.

## Suspected causes

The Agricultural Research Service of the [U.S. Department of Agriculture](https://www.britannica.com/topic/US-Department-of-Agriculture) organized efforts to address the CCD crisis through surveys and data collection, samples analysis, and mitigation and preventive measures. A variety of possible causes of CCD were suggested. They included chemical contamination of colony food stores or [beeswax](https://www.britannica.com/topic/beeswax); poisoning from [pesticides](https://www.britannica.com/technology/pesticide), including nicotine-based insecticides known as [neonicotinoids](https://www.britannica.com/technology/neonicotinoid) (the use of which has been restricted in the [European Union](https://www.britannica.com/topic/European-Union) and certain other countries); possible lack of genetic [diversity](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/diversity) in colonies; and [infection](https://www.britannica.com/science/infection) of colonies by pathogens or parasites, including known honeybee parasites such as the single-celled [microsporidians](https://www.britannica.com/science/microsporidian) (parasitic fungus) *Nosema ceranae* and *N. apis* and the invasive [varroa mite](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Varroa-destructor) (*Varroa destructor*).

Multiple studies have suggested that CCD might be the result of simultaneous exposure to a combination of two or more pathogens or stressors, in which the combination of stressors has a synergistic effect. Bee colonies are commonly found to be infested with pathogens and parasites, and the investigation of the interactions of all the possible causative agents has proved to be a challenge for bee researchers. Pathogens that have been [implicated](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/implicated) in CCD include acute bee paralysis virus, *Ascosphaera apis* (chalkbrood disease), black queen cell virus, chronic bee paralysis virus, deformed wing virus, invertebrate iridescent virus, Israeli acute paralysis virus, Kashmir bee virus, *Nosema* species, *Paenibacillus larvae* (American foulbrood), and sacbrood virus. Many of those pathogens are present in increased abundance in hives affected by CCD, though no pathogens have been found definitively to cause the disorder.

[Brood parasite](https://cdn.britannica.com/96/272596-050-23F80B82/Varroa-destructor-honeybee-parasite-on-bee-pupa.jpg)A young adult varroa mite (*Varroa destructor*) parasitizing a pupal honeybee drone.

Originally from Asia, varroa mites are aggressive parasites of western honeybees that have been implicated in CCD for the stress and [disease](https://www.britannica.com/science/disease) they inflict on affected hives. In addition to the direct harm they exact by feeding on adult and immature bees, varroa mites are capable of transmitting a number of honeybee viruses, including black queen [cell](https://www.britannica.com/science/cell-biology) virus and deformed wing virus. These viruses cause malformations in the bees’ wings, bodies, and legs, and often result in more damage to the bees than the feeding of the mites alone. Ill and weakened bees are more susceptible to CCD, and unfortunately nearly all western honeybee colonies throughout the world are now infected by varroa mites. Eradication is not [feasible](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/feasible), and commercial and hobbyist hives must be carefully managed to keep the [mite](https://www.britannica.com/animal/mite) populations below lethal levels. With few defenses against the novel mite, nearly all unmanaged or feral colonies are killed by varroa mite infections within two or three years, and a severe infestation can wipe out a honeybee colony in a matter of months, though these clear and gradual declines are considered distinct from the sudden colony death and bee disappearances that characterize CCD.

Another parasite that may play a role in CCD is the [phorid fly](https://www.britannica.com/animal/Apocephalus-borealis) *Apocephalus borealis*. A known parasite of [bumblebees](https://www.britannica.com/animal/bumblebee), *A. borealis* has been identified as an emerging threat to honeybees. It was first reported to infect honeybees in 2012, when it was found to be associated with hive abandonment. Honeybees from parasitized hives often are infected with *Nosema* species and deformed wing virus. Many phorid larvae that emerge from dead bees also are infected with those pathogens, suggesting that phorid flies may serve as a reservoir for the agents, transmitting them to bees upon parasitization.

[“Save the Bees” rally](https://cdn.britannica.com/92/238492-050-DC3E7C92/Protestors-against-Neonicotinoid-pesticides-Parliament-Square-London-save-the-bees.jpg)Protesters seeking a ban on neonicotinoid pesticides at a “Save the Bees” rally in England in 2013.

Pesticides implicated in CCD include the [neonicotinoids](https://www.britannica.com/technology/neonicotinoid) imidacloprid, clothianidin, and thiamethoxam. Although no causal link has been established, the agents have been associated with [atypical](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/atypical) honeybee behavior and weakened immune function, which may worsen preexisting parasite and pathogen infections. For example, sublethal exposure to thiamethoxam appears to impair bees’ homing ability, with consequences for colony survival, and exposure to imidacloprid and clothianidin may suppress immune function and promote the proliferation of deformed wing virus in infected honeybees.

In addition, an [antimicrobial agent](https://www.britannica.com/science/antimicrobial-agent) known as fumagillin that is commonly used to control *Nosema* species has been shown to affect the physiology of European honeybees at concentrations that are ineffective against *N. ceranae*, the most invasive of the [microsporidian](https://www.britannica.com/science/microsporidian) pathogens. Because honeybees raised in [brood](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/brood) combs with high levels of pesticide residue show increased susceptibility to *N. ceranae*, there is concern that pesticides and other agents, including fumagillin, could be contributing to an increase in the occurrence of *N. ceranae* infection.

The full sequence of the honeybee genome, which was published in late 2006, was a technological advance that could conceivably help in discovering the underlying cause of CCD in honeybees. Knowledge of the sequence made available new molecular approaches and introduced honeybee [genomics](https://www.britannica.com/science/genomics) to the investigation of CCD. It also enabled scientists to study the impact of the possible causal agents on specific [genes](https://www.britannica.com/science/gene) and honeybee colony [health](https://www.britannica.com/topic/health). Likewise, the advance might help identify new pathogens in honeybees and [unravel](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/unravel) the complex effects of multiple combinations of pathogens and environmental toxins.
