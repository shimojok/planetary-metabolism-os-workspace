### Partnering with the Carbon Industry

The science is clear – there is no path to net zero by 2050 without carbon removal. The most recent IPCC [report](https://www.ipcc.ch/sr15/) indicates that even with significant reductions, which certainly must occur, the world also needs to offset emissions via carbon removal. This will require a much larger market for removal; the United States alone may need to remove the equivalent of [one-third its annual emissions by 2050 every year](https://www.wri.org/initiatives/carbon-removal). While research and innovation in carbon removal looks very promising, and is attracting significant investment dollars, much remains to be done to establish functional global markets, including building clarity on the meaning and measurement of net zero - as outlined in a recent publication in [Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-021-02606-3). This clarity will  help direct these investments into high quality projects, new systems that will collect more data, and ability to provide more transparency for financial and carbon accounting transactions.

This emphasis on data goes well beyond carbon removal markets; improving the quality and quantity of data is necessary to track organizational and global progress toward net zero as we and other organizations take steps to first reduce. For example, Microsoft is taking steps on a data-enabled sustainability transformation with the [Microsoft Cloud for Sustainability](https://blogs.microsoft.com/?p=52559956) which allows organizations to record, report, and reduce their carbon emissions on a path to net zero more effectively. This solution breaks down data silos and removes ambiguity, helping organizations achieve accurate carbon accounting and delivering insights to help them take control of their environmental impact.

With this new system of record for enterprises, it’s become clear that innovation is also needed to develop new capabilities for both carbon reduction and removal credits used to offset emissions. Today, no single system of record exists for carbon credits, but instead many siloed systems that can be difficult to navigate, creating friction in the development of markets. Additionally, these credits were originally built to document carbon avoidance activities and offsets, not removals. That means the data and processes used today for carbon reduction credits will need to be upgraded to identify this new type of carbon removal credit. This presents both a problem and an opportunity. 

So, Azure Global - a team dedicated to helping customers, partners and internal Microsoft teams solve their hardest problems using Microsoft Azure - began to explore this area. What could Azure, our partners, and customers do to drive clarity, quality, and innovation within carbon markets? 

Standing up a new carbon marketplace was not going to solve the problem; however, there is great value in helping evolve the existing market foundation built over 20+ years of dedication. Investing in new carbon removal projects to create new, credible environmental assets should be the focus, which requires digital governance and interoperability standards to compare and evaluate for quality - project-to-project and asset-to-asset. Existing infrastructure is currently siloed within registries that are not integrated and can be difficult to navigate. Evolving market infrastructure should mean registries operate in a common ecosystem based on transparency, which starts with standardizing the ways new asset value is created and transacted.

The first step was clear: given the scope of the problem, we must align to industry. This guided how we think about: 

1.  Quality Carbon Standards – robust requirements for measuring carbon removal, based on approved methodologies or protocols, which results in high-quality carbon removals that builds confidence in the market.
2.  Data Standards - to create carbon removal claims, based on Quality Carbon Standards, that when independently verified, results in a carbon removal credit with common attributes and a transparent linage allowing rapid determination of quality.
3.  Process Standards – for defining the roles and lifecycle for the ecosystem’s participants in which they follow Quality Standards to monitor impact using a common Data standard that is attested as it moves through the process. 
4.  Governance – govern and guide the implementation of these standards together as a functional whole, an iterative process to facilitate improvements and ensure diversity, inclusion and a level playing field.

We decided to start our journey with the open standards body [InterWork Alliance](https://interwork.org/), now an initiative of the [Global Blockchain Business Council (GBBC)](https://gbbcouncil.org/), to lead a task force defining a [Voluntary Ecological Markets Lifecycle](https://interwork.org/wp-content/uploads/2021/05/Voluntary_Ecological_Markets_Overview_Revised.pdf) that includes a common process and data standard. Then, using these process and data standards, we started working with our customers and partners to identify the most critical scenarios to work on, outlined below.

Early results are promising, and showcase possible routes to:

1.  Dramatically increase the supply of carbon removal credits from a diverse ecosystem of projects from around the world.
2.  Decrease the cost of creating these credits by automating and digitizing the lifecycle to lower financial barriers bringing these credits to market.
3.  Speed the time to market and incentive to produce.
4.  Ensure the quality of credits produced, using data standards to allow credits from different sources and methods to easily be understood, compared, sorted, and priced, leading toward market liquidity.

Read on for more information on each scenario and the participants who are helping to identify requirements, build prototypes, and partner to create an ecosystem that will enable high quality carbon removals, based on scientifically verifiable claims in quantities far exceeding what is available today. 

## Voluntary Ecological Market Scenarios

### 1. Scaling Supply – Digitizing the Measurement, Reporting and Verification (MRV) Process

Scaling supply means that organizations creating and governing new science-based protocols for making carbon removal claims will need to work with those organizations independently verifying them. The lifecycle requires that an ecological project create an ecological claim following a quality standard for the type of activity that will be the source of the claim. Meaning an ecological claim following a carbon removal quality standard would become a carbon removal claim; this can also be generally applied to other types of quality standards for carbon reduction, water, etc. The ecological claim itself is comprised of a lot of data that is treated as “evidence” supporting the claim that can be independently verified. 

![Process Flow](https://media-exp1.licdn.com/dms/image/C4D12AQHyl3D4ZzCJHQ/article-inline_image-shrink_1500_2232/0/1635904164459?e=1666828800&v=beta&t=eQBzfQGzTJ00yf8iFa_xTlLgHd7oQKGoluV3gMi6Yag)

The evidence itself can be from multiple sources, be completely different across claims, and even include proprietary, confidential data, or potentially reference another data source. But the collection, chain of custody and metadata of this evidence is standardized and can reuse the same infrastructure across many projects and verifiers. We believe that shared infrastructure using common data and process standards, designed with interchangeable quality standards for claims will speed the time to market, lower the cost of development and increase the quality of these removals, ultimately increasing the value of the carbon claim. 

![Supply logos](https://media-exp1.licdn.com/dms/image/C4D12AQGLzVEnD9MWtA/article-inline_image-shrink_1500_2232/0/1635904182910?e=1666828800&v=beta&t=T1DCxR5BFmAyAyDREQylKQW0SLnuUdOlxCXnppGtvtE)

We worked with a wide range of organizations, including [Aker Carbon Capture](https://akercarboncapture.com/), [Carbon Asset Solutions](https://www.carbonassetsolutions.com/), [ClimateCHECK](https://www.climate-check.com/), [ESMC](https://ecosystemservicesmarket.org/), [Gold Standard](https://www.goldstandard.org/), [Ørsted](https://orsted.com/), [Patch](https://www.patch.io/), [Puro.earth](https://puro.earth/), [SustainCERT](https://www.sustain-cert.com/) and [Verra](https://verra.org/) as well as the [Nation of Colombia](https://www.minambiente.gov.co/cambio-climatico-y-gestion-del-riesgo/renare/) to test these process and data standards using shared Azure infrastructure. The initial results for voluntary markets look promising but will take some time to gauge.  A Digital Measurement, Reporting and Verification (MRV) framework was created as a byproduct and is [available now](https://interwork.org/resources/mrv-framework/) at the Interwork Alliance for further development and standardization.

### 2. Marketplace

Following the Data and Process Standards generates a common representation of carbon removals across project types, which allows for rapid quality, value, and preference comparisons for buyers. However, discovery of these removals at scale will require marketplaces, and eventually exchanges, to match buyers and sellers. Exploring how buyers find removals based on their preferences with attributes like durability, additionality, source region, removal type and a wide range of co-benefits will require marketplaces designed to service this demand.

![No alt text provided for this image](https://media-exp1.licdn.com/dms/image/C4D12AQGMtOwZZFOaVQ/article-inline_image-shrink_1000_1488/0/1635904216527?e=1666828800&v=beta&t=ojxIaqTCL2uv2GHi6mgpt2vC_XBjtnEJf75BG0R-arc)

We worked with [Climate Impact X](https://www.climateimpactx.com/) (CIX), [European Energy Exchange](https://www.eex.com/) (EEX), [Nasdaq](https://www.nasdaq.com/), [Patch](https://www.patch.io/), [Puro.earth](https://puro.earth/) and [Xpansiv](https://xpansiv.com/) to evaluate and test marketplace connections to this shared infrastructure for carbon credits. These partners are working on building the marketplaces and exchanges suitable for scaling the voluntary carbon market and recognize the need for a shift towards removal credits while also supporting existing reduction credits.

### 3. Trading and Settlement

For this ecosystem to scale quickly, carbon removal producers need to be able to monetize their credits quickly, which means cash in the hands of suppliers with less friction along the way. Matching buyers and sellers via marketplaces and exchanges to transfer ownership of a removal is only one half of the transaction; the other half is to provide cash settlement from buyer to seller. An ecosystem like this would need to be able to scale purchasing to millions of households and businesses, offering payments from debit/credit cards to corporate payment systems. We believe that this shared ecosystem should allow settlements to be near real-time, putting cash in the hands of suppliers faster and with less friction. 

![Project Carbon logos](https://media-exp1.licdn.com/dms/image/C4D12AQEVwozcSXYJOA/article-inline_image-shrink_1000_1488/0/1635904236887?e=1666828800&v=beta&t=adEQjhKizLefdI6jbGLxECriXEGhzXg66VkNvjxkUJM)

We worked with [Project Carbon](https://www.globalprojectcarbon.com/), an international joint effort from [CIBC](https://www.cibc.com/), [Itaú](https://www.itau.com/), [National Australia Bank](https://www.nab.com.au/)and [NatWest Group](https://www.natwestgroup.com/), to connect its network of buyers to this shared infrastructure to simulate an end-to-end trade with both the transfer and cash segments of the transaction. Project Carbon is a digitized settlement platform that leverages the banks’ trusted ability to reduce counterparty risk, scale client onboarding quickly and provide a book of record of transactions, supporting a thriving global platform for quality carbon credits with clear and consistent pricing and standards. The integration of supply and distribution looks very promising, and we hope that it brings climate action within reach of every person and corporation.

### 4. Financial Ecosystem Integration

A new carbon credit ecosystem should allow for owners of credits to hold on to their assets to trade them again or to retire them when needed to lower their effective emissions. It should enable credits to be bundled into new products where the credit can be included to establish a net-zero product. From fuels to fruit, these carbon credits can be attached to commodities and finished goods to nest climate friendly products into the supply chain.

![BNYM Logo](https://media-exp1.licdn.com/dms/image/C4D12AQH-AqXB0AjpnQ/article-inline_image-shrink_1500_2232/0/1635904290458?e=1666828800&v=beta&t=VcNv5djs8zW8SZGQYZBDSFRt2HXuOgBc2V_HN6WvHb8)

We are working with [BNY Mellon](https://www.bnymellon.com/) to explore how carbon credits fit into the financial ecosystem. Our hope is that by integrating carbon credits in both the traditional and digital asset markets, this may increase access to institutional investors and help them to achieve their environmental and sustainability goals.

We are just getting started on this “all hands-on deck” effort as a part of our three priorities – meaning, measurement and markets.
