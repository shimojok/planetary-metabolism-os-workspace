# Soils should have rights too

#climatechange #fao 

###### A pioneer in soil research and Conservation Agriculture, Dr. Rattan Lal paved the way in one of FAO’s key areas of work

[2](http://www.fao.org/sustainable-development-goals/goals/goal-2/en/)

[3](http://www.fao.org/sustainable-development-goals/goals/goal-3/en/)

[15](http://www.fao.org/sustainable-development-goals/goals/goal-15/en/)

Lal laid the foundations in Conservation Agriculture, an approach that can help restore the world’s soils, capture carbon and combat food insecurity. ©Ohio State University/K. Chamberlain

01/12/2020

Growing up on a small farm in India, Dr. Rattan Lal experienced much of the hardship faced by today’s smallholder farmers. His family didn’t have running water or electricity, but he explains, “We never missed it because that was the way everybody lived.”

インドの小さな農場で育ったRattanLal博士は、今日の小規模農家が直面する困難の多くを経験しました。 彼の家族は水道も電気も持っていませんでしたが、彼は「それがみんなの生き方だったので、私たちはそれを見逃すことはありませんでした」と説明します。

As a farmer, his father struggled with irrigation challenges and the related issue of saline soil, both of which Lal later realised could be remedied by improving drainage of the land. But it wasn’t until after years of studying that he began to understand and teach the world about the fundamental role of soil.

農民として、彼の父親は灌漑の課題とそれに関連する塩性土壌の問題に苦しんでいましたが、ラルは後に土地の排水を改善することで改善できることに気づきました。 しかし、彼が土壌の基本的な役割について世界に理解し、教え始めたのは、何年にもわたる研究の後でした。

In fact, a Nobel Peace Prize Certificate* and a [World Food Prize](https://www.worldfoodprize.org/en/laureates/2020_lal/) later, Lal has changed the way we view sustainable soil management and has trodden new ground in what we now call Conservation Agriculture (CA), an approach that can play a huge role in restoring the world’s neglected soils, capturing carbon and combating food insecurity.

実際、ノーベル平和賞証明書*と[世界食糧賞]（https://www.worldfoodprize.org/en/laureates/2020_lal/）の後、ラルは持続可能な土壌管理の見方を変え、新しいものを踏みにじりました。 私たちが現在「保全農業」と呼んでいるものに基づいて、世界の無視された土壌を回復し、炭素を捕獲し、食糧不安と戦うのに大きな役割を果たすことができるアプローチです。

At its core, CA minimises soil disturbance (which means reducing tilling), provides permanent soil cover and diversifies crop production, all of which can contribute to enhancing biodiversity, reducing water use and improving soil health.

CAは、その中核として、土壌の乱れを最小限に抑え（耕作を減らすことを意味します）、永続的な土壌被覆を提供し、作物生産を多様化します。これらはすべて、生物多様性の向上、水の使用量の削減、土壌の健康の改善に貢献できます。

Conservation Agriculture plays an important part in how FAO supports small-scale farmers in Africa, Asia, Latin America and the Caribbean. The benefits of this approach are numerous, from increased yields and reduced costs for farmers to healthier soils and greater carbon sequestration. Lal came to understand all of this and has since endeavoured to spread the word.

保全農業は、FAOがアフリカ、アジア、ラテンアメリカ、カリブ海の小規模農家を支援する方法において重要な役割を果たしています。 このアプローチの利点は、農民の収穫量の増加とコストの削減から、より健康的な土壌とより多くの炭素隔離に至るまで、数多くあります。 ラルはこれらすべてを理解するようになり、それ以来、その言葉を広めるよう努めてきました。

**A pioneer**

In the 1970s, when Lal was working for the International Institute of Tropical Agriculture (IITA) in Nigeria, he discovered that by eradicating ploughing practices and simply covering the soil with crop residues he was able to produce five tonnes of maize per hectare every season; local farmers could only manage one tonne. Yet, for some reason, they still wouldn’t adopt CA practices.

1970年代、ナイジェリアの国際熱帯農業研究所（IITA）で働いていたとき、彼は耕作を根絶し、作物残渣で土壌を覆うだけで、季節ごとに1ヘクタールあたり5トンのトウモロコシを生産できることを発見しました。 地元の農家は1トンしか管理できませんでした。 それでも、何らかの理由で、彼らはまだCAの慣行を採用しませんでした。

He put this down to a distrust in no-till agriculture. Over forty years later, FAO and other organisations like the African Conservation Tillage Network (ACT) are scaling up CA practices in Africa with much more success.

彼はこれを不耕起農業への不信に置きました。  40年以上後、FAOやAfrican Conservation Tillage Network（ACT）のような他の組織は、アフリカでのCAの実践を拡大し、より多くの成功を収めています。

For instance, through the project [Strengthening coordination, scaling up and governance of Conservation Agriculture in Southern Africa,](https://www.fao.org/africa/news/detail-news/en/c/1302181/) FAO is increasing the adoption of CA among farmers.

たとえば、プロジェクト[南アフリカにおける保全農業の調整、スケールアップ、ガバナンスの強化]（https://www.fao.org/africa/news/detail-news/en/c/1302181/）を通じて、FAOは 農民の間でCAの採用を増やす。

Given that this region is warming at twice the global rate, there is a pressing need to strengthen food systems and increase the resilience of farmers, many of whom practice either small-scale or subsistence farming. To do so, the project is boosting collaboration between governments, NGOs and development agencies, meaning that FAO and its partners are better able to document and share CA knowledge and best practices with farmers.

この地域が世界の2倍の速度で温暖化していることを考えると、食料システムを強化し、小規模または自給自足農業を実践している農民の回復力を高めることが急務となっています。 そのために、このプロジェクトは政府、NGO、開発機関間のコラボレーションを促進しています。つまり、FAOとそのパートナーは、CAの知識とベストプラクティスを文書化し、農家と共有することができます。

A recent example of such a collaboration comes from Zimbabwe, where FAO and the NGO, Foundations for Farming, have been supporting the government in its ambitious target of training 1.8 million farmers in CA.

“I see no reason why Africa cannot become the breadbasket of the world,” Lal says.

このようなコラボレーションの最近の例は、FAOとNGOであるFoundations for Farmingが、CAで180万人の農民を訓練するという野心的な目標で政府を支援しているジンバブエからのものです。

 「アフリカが世界の穀倉地帯になれない理由はわかりません」とラルは言います。

**Restoring neglected soils**

Over many years, soils have been neglected: monocropping and the improper use of chemicals have depleted the essential nutrients and microbes found in healthy soils. To reverse this trend, Lal insists that farmers should first manage soil fertility through organic matter.

“Farmers don’t return what they take away and consequently soils are degraded. People are a mirror image of the soil they live upon: their health is also equally affected.”

When it comes to land use, he insists that we have more than we need – we just don’t use it properly.

“This idea of producing more and more and wasting more and more is a crime against nature. The ideal philosophy is producing more from less: less land, less water, less fertilizer, less pesticide, less greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions, less wastage.”

Although he admits that soil and CA alone will not offset GHG emissions, “Restoring carbon in soil and trees is the lowest hanging fruit and has many core benefits: food and nutrition security, improved water quality and greater biodiversity.” 

“If we employ science-based practices, no-till, cover cropping and CA,” he says, “agriculture has to be a solution to climate change.”

**Wide-eyed optimism**

Ultimately, Lal believes the role of soil is far too fundamental to ignore.

“Soil is a living thing: 25 percent of all biodiversity is in soil,” he says. “And like any living thing, soil should also have right to be protected, restored and managed properly.”

Lal is incredibly optimistic: he’s glad to see a growing appreciation of soil and praises international efforts to combat climate change.

“FAO and other organisations are now recognising the importance of soil: my recent [[World Food Prize] award](https://www.worldfoodprize.org/en/laureates/2020_lal/) is testimony enough to this.”

As the climate changes rapidly in many parts of the world, FAO is encouraging the adoption of more resilient and efficient production systems such as CA. If the world’s small-scale farmers are to achieve food security, soils must play a key role. This is why FAO promotes sustainable soil management through initiatives such as the [Global Soil Partnership](https://www.fao.org/global-soil-partnership/en/) and celebrates [World Soil Day](https://www.fao.org/world-soil-day/en/) – held annually on 5 December – to raise awareness on the importance of soil resources.

_* Dr Rattan Lal was recognized with a Nobel Peace Prize Certificate for his contributions to the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC), the Nobel Peace Prize co-laureate in 2007._
